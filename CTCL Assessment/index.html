Elected Prosecutors

General Insights

Ninety-five percent of elected prosecutors in the U.S. are white, and 82 percent are men, according to data from the Center for Technology and Civic Life. More striking is that 73 percent are white men, while around just 1 percent of elected prosecutors are women of color. There is just one American Indian/Alaska Native elected prosecutor.

Ninety-one percent of attorney generals, states’ highest law enforcement office, are white. There is just one black attorney general, two Latino, and one multiracial. Narrowing down to county prosecutors, an overwhelming 98 percent are white, and nearly 80 percent are white men. These data do not indicate the demographics of each state, but that should be analyzed as well to judge representation better.

More than half of the 46 states that elect prosecutors have no black elected prosecutors, including Illinois, Florida and Massachusetts. Thirteen have no elected prosecutors of color at all, like Connecticut and Colorado.

Geographic Insights

Oklahoma is the only state who has an elected American Indian/Alaska Native prosecutor in the country.

The western and northwestern United States have a low concentration of elected prosecutors of color. Many western states, like Washington and Idaho, have none at all. Just 2.7 percent of elected prosecutors in Oregon are not white.

This contrasts with states in the south, who generally see a relatively higher percentage of elected officials who aren’t white. Five of New Mexico’s 14 elected prosecutors (about 36 percent) are Hispanic or Latino, according to the data. Virginia and Mississippi also have fairly high percentages, with 16.5 percent and 22.4 percent respectively.

Who to Reach Out to

Based on the data and insights presented above, it is recommend CTCL reach out to:


  • The Southern Poverty Law Center. Although the SPLC is largely known for it's work documenting hate crimes, it often reports on issues of race and representation.
  • The National Black Prosecutors Association. The NBPA is a nonprofit that advocates for and connects black prosecutors.
  • The Equal Justice Intiative. Headquartered in Montgomery, AL, the EJI provides legal representation to marginalized communities.
